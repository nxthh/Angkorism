Temple Details

1. Bayon Temple – The Smiling Heart of Angkor Thom

Bayon Temple is one of the most recognizable landmarks in the Angkor Archaeological Park. Built in the late 12th century by King Jayavarman VII, this temple served as the spiritual center of Angkor Thom, the last great capital of the Khmer Empire.

What makes Bayon extraordinary are its 216 giant stone faces carved into 54 towers. These serene smiles are believed to represent either Avalokiteshvara, the Buddhist bodhisattva of compassion, or a symbolic image of King Jayavarman VII himself. Walking through Bayon feels like being watched peacefully from every direction.

2. Ta Prohm – Where Nature and History Collide

Ta Prohm is famous worldwide for its haunting beauty and dramatic atmosphere. Unlike many Angkor temples that were restored, Ta Prohm was intentionally left in a semi-ruined condition to preserve its mystical jungle appearance.

Massive silk-cotton and strangler fig trees wrap themselves around temple walls, doorways, and roofs, creating a breathtaking fusion of stone and nature. The site became internationally famous after appearing in the film “Tomb Raider,” but its real magic lies in showing how nature slowly reclaims human creations.

3. Banteay Srei – The Jewel of Khmer Art

Banteay Srei is often described as the most beautiful temple in Cambodia. Built in the 10th century and dedicated to the Hindu god Shiva, this temple is smaller than Angkor Wat but unmatched in artistic detail.

Constructed from pink sandstone, Banteay Srei features some of the finest carvings in Khmer architecture. Floral motifs, mythological scenes, and delicate reliefs are carved so deeply and precisely that many still appear sharp after more than a thousand years.

4. Preah Khan – The Sacred Sword

Preah Khan was built by Jayavarman VII in honor of his father and functioned as much more than a temple. It served as a university, monastery, and administrative center.

Historical inscriptions suggest the complex once housed thousands of monks, scholars, dancers, and officials. Its endless corridors, hidden courtyards, and partially collapsed chambers make exploration feel like discovering a lost city.

5. Phnom Kulen – Birthplace of the Khmer Empire

Phnom Kulen is a sacred mountain located northeast of Angkor. In 802 AD, King Jayavarman II declared independence from Java here and proclaimed himself the universal monarch, marking the symbolic birth of the Khmer Empire.

Today, Phnom Kulen is known for waterfalls, reclining Buddha statues, and sacred river carvings. It remains an important pilgrimage destination for Cambodians and offers a refreshing natural escape from temple exploration.

6. Kbal Spean – The River of a Thousand Lingas

Hidden deep in the jungle, Kbal Spean is an archaeological site unlike any other. Instead of buildings, its sacred carvings are found directly in the riverbed and surrounding rocks.

Hundreds of Shiva lingas, lotus symbols, and Hindu deities were carved into the sandstone to spiritually bless the river water flowing toward Angkor. Reaching Kbal Spean requires a jungle hike, making the journey itself part of the adventure.

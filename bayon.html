Bayon Temple – The Smiling Heart of Angkor Thom

Bayon Temple stands at the exact center of Angkor Thom, the final and most ambitious capital of the Khmer Empire. Built during the late 12th and early 13th centuries under the reign of King Jayavarman VII, Bayon was more than a temple—it was a political and spiritual statement. Unlike Angkor Wat’s symmetrical perfection, Bayon feels mysterious, complex, and almost alive.

Its most iconic feature is the collection of giant stone faces carved onto towering structures. A total of 216 serene smiling faces look outward in all directions, creating the strange feeling that the temple is watching over every visitor. Historians still debate their identity. Some believe they represent Avalokiteshvara, the Buddhist bodhisattva of compassion, while others argue they are idealized portraits of Jayavarman VII himself.

Walking through Bayon is like entering a maze of history. Narrow corridors reveal intricate bas-reliefs depicting ancient Khmer daily life, military victories, market scenes, religious rituals, and even naval warfare. Every wall tells a story, making Bayon one of the most important historical documents carved in stone in Southeast Asia.
